01 · The Problem

A content team punching below its weight

When I joined SimplePractice in 2022, the content operation was producing a fraction of what the market opportunity demanded. At that time, the platform served 45,000 mental health professionals — a highly specific, skeptical audience of clinicians, psychiatrists, and therapists who wouldn't tolerate generic health content. The brief was ambitious: build a credibility-first content engine that could drive significant organic growth and qualified lead volume without sacrificing editorial quality for the audience we were serving.

There was no AI workflow. No contributor framework. No editorial standards document. No measurement system connecting content to pipeline. Just a small team, a big audience, and a mandate to build.

03 · The System

How it actually worked

The core insight: at this scale and for this audience, you can't manage content. You have to build a machine that manages itself — with clear inputs, a defined process, quality gates, and feedback loops baked in from the start.

Editorial Pipeline Architecture
Strategy
SEO-led editorial calendar built around keyword clusters, audience intent, and product priorities. Prioritized topics with high practitioner search volume and commercial intent over vanity traffic. Calendar owned weekly editorial meetings with GTM, Product Marketing, and Customer Success.

Keyword research Intent mapping Cross-functional alignment
Intake
AI-augmented brief generation using Gemini Gems — custom AI models trained on SimplePractice's voice, audience, and editorial standards. Briefs included: target keyword, audience segment, clinical accuracy requirements, word count, internal links, and a suggested outline. Reduced brief-writing time by ~70%.

Gemini Gems Custom AI models Templatized briefs
Production
Tiered contributor network of 150+ freelancers segmented by expertise: licensed clinicians for clinical accuracy, SEO specialists for high-volume keyword content, senior editors for thought leadership and executive ghostwriting. Each tier had a defined brief format, rate card, revision policy, and quality rubric. Onboarding included brand immersion, voice training, and editorial standards certification.

Tiered contributors Clinical SMEs Onboarding system
Quality
Multi-gate editorial review: AI-assisted first-pass for voice/tone/SEO compliance, editor review for clinical accuracy and narrative quality, final gatekeeper review before publication. I acted as the final gatekeeper — making judgment calls on what ships, what gets killed, and what needs rework. If it could have been published by any other platform, it didn't ship.

Editorial rubrics Clinical review Voice compliance
Distribution
Multi-channel distribution playbook — every piece of content had a defined downstream life: blog publication, email newsletter, social adaptation, sales enablement asset, or customer story. Partnered with GTM and Communications to align high-value content with product launches and campaigns.

Blog Email Social Sales enablement
Measurement
Content attribution framework tracking organic traffic, keyword rankings, time-on-page, lead conversion by content type, and paid account attribution. Weekly performance reviews fed directly back into editorial prioritization. Content that converted got scaled; content that didn't got cut or refreshed.

04 · The AI Layer

How AI changed the operation

AI didn't replace the editorial operation — it redesigned it. By 2025, 75% of content touched AI at some point in the workflow. But the AI was always a tool in a human-designed system, not a replacement for editorial judgment.

The key innovation was building custom Gemini Gems — AI models fine-tuned on SimplePractice's voice, clinical terminology, and audience expectations. These weren't generic prompts. They were trained editorial collaborators that understood the difference between how a therapist talks to a client and how a platform talks to a therapist.

The result: a lean two-person in-house team that operated at the output level of a team three times its size — without sacrificing the clinical credibility that made the content worth reading in the first place.

05 · What I Learned

Lessons from building at scale

01
The bottleneck is almost always the brief, not the writer. When content quality is inconsistent, the instinct is to edit more. Usually the real problem is that the brief wasn't specific enough. Investing in brief quality upstream is the highest-leverage editorial intervention.
02
Treat contributors like partners, not vendors. The best freelancers in the network weren't just delivering copy — they were invested in the product. That only happens when onboarding is real, feedback is fast, and the best people get recurring work that feels meaningful.
03
AI exposes weak editorial judgment, it doesn't replace it. Teams that struggled with AI tools weren't struggling with the technology — they were struggling with clarity about what good looks like. The AI made that ambiguity visible faster.
04
Credibility is a moat. In a space where every health platform was producing generic wellness content, choosing clinical depth over volume created a durable competitive advantage. The audience noticed. The algorithm noticed too.
